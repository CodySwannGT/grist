# Story, mystery & structure

> **Spoiler-bearing.** This is the full through-line, including the ending. For
> the spoiler-free version, read [pitch](pitch.md). Setting in [world](world.md),
> cast in [characters](characters.md).

## The central mystery (slow burn, in layers)

The plot is a single question — *what really happened the night the gods went
silent, and what is the slow rot of the world?* — peeled one layer at a time over
40+ hours. Nothing is rushed; each layer recontextualizes the last.

- **Layer 0 — the hook.** Wren's smuggling job is a *person*: a sleeping girl
  (Sable) the great Houses will burn Vanta to recover. Why is one sleeping girl
  worth a city?
- **Layer 1 — the asset.** Sable produces grist without being rendered. The
  Houses don't want her dead; they want her *farmed*. What is she?
- **Layer 2 — the supply chain.** What grist actually is. The player follows the
  fuel back to its source and learns the Marrow runs on the dead — that the
  comfortable world is powered by rendered souls, including people's.
- **Layer 3 — the original sin.** The Sundering was not an accident. The founding
  Houses **murdered the Choir** to start the grist industry. Civilization is built
  on deicide. (Maren holds fragments; the party corroborates them across regions.)
- **Layer 4 — the dreaming god.** AUREL did not fully die. It has been dying
  *slowly*, for nine hundred years, and the world's entropy — the fading Weave,
  the dimming colors — is its death-rattle. Sable is its last coherent fragment:
  an heir, not a weapon.
- **Layer 5 — the choice.** The endgame is not "stop the villain and restore the
  world." It is deciding what a dying world's death *means*. (See endings.)

## The villain's clock

Running under the mystery, **Mr. Sallow** is executing his own plan (see
[characters](characters.md)). The player keeps brushing against its edges before
understanding it. At the midpoint he acts.

## Act structure — one open world, mourned twice

GRIST rebuilds FFVI's *World of Balance → World of Ruin* turn as an **open world
that transforms**: the same map, lived in and then grieved.

### Act I — The Reach (≈ hours 0–18)

The open world as a living dystopia: Vanta's explorable tiers (the Crown, the
Tiers, the Marrow, the Deep) and the hinterlands beyond. The player wanders,
takes side-stories, gathers the party one damaged person at a time, and walks the
mystery up through Layer 3. Tone: neon-noir with a creeping dread underneath.
Climax: the truth of the Sundering and Sable's nature lands — and Sallow makes his
move.

### The Reckoning — the world-turn (the midpoint)

Sallow triggers the **Second Sundering**: he overloads AUREL's corpse-reactor to
render a vast swath of the Reach at once. A region — and much of lower Vanta — is
liquidated. The party is broken and scattered; some are lost, some changed. The
open world tips into its second state.

### Act II — Ashfall (≈ hours 18–40+)

The same world, magic-dead and haunted: a broken Vanta, ash where regions were,
the Weave guttering toward dark. Act II is more open and nonlinear — in true FFVI
fashion, the party is scattered and you **reassemble whoever you can find**, each
reunion a self-contained story, and you can march on the ending with an
incomplete party if you choose. The stakes shift from *stop a conspiracy* to
*decide a dying world's fate*. Side content deepens into elegy. Climax: descend to
AUREL's heart, confront Sallow, and make the ending-choice.

## Pacing for 40+ hours (the "no rush" design)

- **The main quest is gated by deliberate beats, not urgency.** There is rarely a
  ticking clock in the fiction; the world invites you to stay, wander, and listen.
- **Side-stories are the point.** The mourning, the texture, and the best
  character writing live off the main path. Completionists comfortably exceed 40
  hours; the critical path still satisfies near it.
- **The mystery is environmental.** Much of the truth is *found*, not delivered —
  in ruins, ledgers, Sidhe memory, and the testimony of the Bound — so attention
  is rewarded and the slow burn stays in the player's hands.
- **Grist is the grind.** The fuel that runs the world is also the player's
  currency and progression resource — so the deliberate, accumulative grind the
  pitch promises is the same substance as the theme. Mechanics *are* the argument.

## Set-pieces

In the FFVI tradition of the unforgettable spectacle scene (the opera), GRIST
plants signature set-pieces — chief among them a **Sidhe requiem-rite** in the
Deep, where the last of the Withered sing a fragment of the old Choir-song and a
key layer of the mystery breaks open in the middle of the music. (See
[themes-and-tone](themes-and-tone.md).)

## Endings (the weight of the slow burn)

The climax refuses an easy resolution. Through Sable, the dreaming god's heir, the
party can:

- **Wake the god.** Restore the Weave and the age of wonders — but a wakened AUREL
  is not the world's servant, and the cost of a living god is its own kind of
  dread. Magic returns; mastery of it does not.
- **Let it die.** Allow AUREL to finish dying in peace. The grist economy
  collapses with its source; the world becomes mortal, magic-poor, uncertain — but
  *unowned*, no longer fuel for anyone. The hardest, quietest, most hopeful end.
- **Finish the Sundering** (Sallow's end). Render everything to the clean silence
  before the song. Oblivion as mercy. Available, and damning.
- **A third way** — mortal stewardship: refuse both god and oblivion, break the
  Houses, and leave the Reach to its small, unmagical people. *The time of the
  gods is over; the time of mortals begins.* The LOTR note, fully sounded.

Which ends are reachable depends on who the party became across 40 hours — whose
stories were finished, what truths were faced, what the player chose to mourn and
what they chose to fight for.
