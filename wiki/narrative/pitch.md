# GRIST — the pitch

## Logline

A thousand years ago the gods were murdered so their corpses could be refined
into fuel. The world has run on that fuel ever since. **GRIST** is the slow,
mournful, open-world JRPG about the people who discover where the fuel comes
from — and have to decide whether a dying world should be saved, finished, or
finally allowed to grow up.

## The one-line hook

*The city runs on refined souls, and a murdered god is dreaming beneath it.*

## What it is

GRIST is, in its bones, **Final Fantasy VI for a modern audience**: an ensemble
cast with no single hero, a world built on industrialized magic, a
world-shattering catastrophe at the midpoint that splits the game into two
distinct world-states, and a theatrical, nihilistic antagonist who doesn't want
to conquer the world so much as *close it down*.

What makes it new is the fusion: it takes FFVI's "magitek" — magic married to
machinery — and runs it all the way to its honest conclusion. If magic is a
resource, someone owns the mines. If the gods are real, someone has put them on
a balance sheet. The result is **Cyberpunk 2077's corporate dystopia grown over
the elegiac, fading high fantasy of The Lord of the Rings** — chrome grafted
onto rune, neon over ancient stone, a world where the age of wonders didn't end
in glory but in liquidation.

## Why a publisher should greenlight it

- **It owns the most commercial fusion in the genre right now.** Dark fantasy ×
  cyberpunk is the lane of *Final Fantasy XVI*'s tone, *Cyberpunk 2077*'s
  setting-appeal, and *Elden Ring*'s mournful open world. GRIST stands in the
  middle of all three with a single, legible identity.
- **It has a thesis you can put on a poster.** The title *is* the pitch. "Grist
  for the mill" — the world, the people, and the gods are all raw material for
  the machine. Every system, from the economy to the combat, expresses it.
- **It is structurally proven.** The FFVI two-world turn (a living world that is
  catastrophically broken at the midpoint) is one of the most beloved structures
  in JRPG history. GRIST rebuilds it as an *open* world that transforms — the
  same map, mourned twice.
- **It is a slow burn by design, and that's the product.** This is a 40+ hour
  game that respects patience: an unhurried central mystery, environmental
  storytelling, and side-stories that are the point, not filler. It is built for
  the audience that finished *Disco Elysium* and *Elden Ring* and wanted to
  *stay longer*.
- **It has franchise and aesthetic upside.** A deep, named cosmology (the Choir,
  the Weave, the Sundering, the Bound), a merchandisable look (chrome + rune,
  gold + ash), a memorable villain, and a multi-ending climax that leaves a
  living world to expand into.

## The elevator version

> You are a courier in the undercity of Vanta — a megacity grown into the corpse
> of a dead god. A routine smuggling job turns out to be a *person*: a sleeping
> girl the great Houses will burn the city to recover. Finding out why she
> matters will teach you what fuel really is, what happened the night the gods
> went silent, and that the slow rot everyone calls progress is a god taking a
> thousand years to die. Then, halfway in, a quiet little man in a grey suit
> decides it's time to balance the books — and the world ends for the second
> time. The rest is up to whoever's left.
